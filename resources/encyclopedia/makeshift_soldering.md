# SURVIVAL MECHANICS: Makeshift Soldering

## Core Concept
Joining two electrical wires using solder (a fusible metal alloy), but heating the connection using a piece of metal heated in an open fire instead of an electric soldering iron.

## Visual & Physical Mechanics for Prompt Generation

When generating video prompts involving makeshift soldering, adhere strictly to these mechanical realities:

### 1. Preparation of the Wires
- **Stripping & Twisting:** The plastic insulation must be stripped back. The bare copper or silver wires of the two ends are tightly twisted together into a single rigid joint.
- **No Floating Solder:** The thin thread of soldering wire (salvaged from the plane) is tightly wrapped around the cold, twisted copper wire joint *before* heat is applied. This frees up the survivor's hands.

### 2. The Makeshift "Iron"
- **The Tool:** A piece of solid, thermally conductive metal (e.g., a steel skewer, a thick nail, a screwdriver blade from the aircraft's tool kit). It must have a handle or be held with thick cloth to prevent burning the hand.
- **The Heat Source:** The tip of this metal tool is held inside the hottest part of a small campfire or improvised fuel fire until it is extremely hot (potentially glowing dull red in low light, or visibly scorched).

### 3. The Application of Heat
- **The Mechanics:** The hot tip of the metal tool is pressed firmly against the **copper wires themselves**, NOT directly against the solder thread. 
- **The Flow:** The heat transfers from the hot tool -> into the copper wires -> which then melt the wrapped solder from the inside out. The solder will visually "flow" or wick into the tiny gaps between the twisted wire strands, changing from a wire shape into a shiny metallic liquid coating.
- **Visuals:** Expect slight wisps of smoke as residual oils or impurities on the wire burn off. No massive sparks or arc flashes (the circuit is not live). 

### 4. Post-Soldering
- **Cooling:** The joint must not be touched or moved while it cools, or the metal will crystallize and weaken (a "cold joint").
- **Insulation:** The survivor must wrap the exposed shiny joint in electrical tape or improvised insulation (like melting a small piece of plastic over it) to prevent a short circuit.

## Common AI Hallucinations to Prevent
- **Arc Welding Sparks:** AI often confuses soldering with arc welding, generating a shower of sparks and blue blinding light. Soldering is quiet, slow, and produces only a tiny wisp of smoke.
- **Melting the Solder Directly:** The AI might show the metal tool melting the solder thread in mid-air and letting it drip onto the wire. This creates a weak mechanical bond. The wires must be heated to melt the solder.
- **Bare Hands on Scorching Metal:** The survivor must hold the heated tool from a safe distance or use an improvised insulated grip.
