# Technical Guide: Carving a Large-Scale Tenon Joint on a Log

This guide details the physical process of carving a functional, large-scale tenon on the end of a round log using traditional hand tools. The focus is on mechanical accuracy and realism to inform video generation.

---

## 1.0 Tools & Materials

### 1.1 Required Tools
*   **Crosscut Saw:** A large, two-person or one-person crosscut saw for severing the log to length and making the shoulder cuts.
*   **Rip Saw:** A robust handsaw with rip teeth for cutting the tenon cheeks (with the grain). A large tenon saw is a good example.
*   **Framing Chisels & Slicks:** Heavy-duty, large chisels (e.g., 2-inch width) for rough waste removal and shaping. A slick is a very large, long-handled chisel used for paring and finishing large surfaces.
*   **Mallet:** A heavy wooden mallet for driving the chisels.
*   **Marking Tools:**
    *   Chalk line for snapping straight lines along the length of the log.
    *   Large framing square or combination square.
    *   Marking knife or sharp pencil for precise lines.
    *   Flexible ruler or metal banding to wrap around the log for marking shoulder lines.
*   **Log Securing Equipment:** A log scribe or sturdy sawhorses with chocks or a purpose-built log holder to prevent any movement.

### 1.2 Tool Handling & Grip

*   **Crosscut Saw:**
    *   **Grip:** The primary hand grips the main handle, with the index finger often extended along the side for steering. The off-hand rests lightly on the log for balance.
    *   **Stance:** A balanced, wide stance with feet firmly planted. The body should be aligned with the saw cut, allowing the arm and shoulder to move like a piston.
*   **Chisel & Mallet:**
    *   **Chisel Grip:** One hand holds the chisel handle firmly, while the other hand can either hold the mallet or guide the blade. For precise paring, the dominant hand holds the handle, and the non-dominant hand grips the steel shank near the cutting edge, with fingers pressing it down for control.
    *   **Mallet Grip:** The mallet is held with a firm but not tense grip, allowing for a full arm swing to deliver solid, square blows to the chisel handle.
    *   **Body Mechanics:** The body should be positioned to drive the chisel away from oneself. Use body weight to assist in the push, especially with a slick.

## 2.0 Step-by-Step Process

### 2.1 Preparation & Marking Out

The log must be immovably secured before any work begins.

*   **Visual Appearance:** The log is rough, with intact bark or a peeled, uneven surface. It is not perfectly cylindrical.
*   **Physical Movements:**
    1.  **Establish Centerlines:** The user finds the approximate center on both ends of the log. They then use a chalk line to snap vertical and horizontal reference lines across the end grain and along the top and sides of the log. These lines serve as the primary reference for all subsequent marks.
    2.  **Marking the Shoulder Line:** This is the most critical marking step. The user wraps a flexible ruler or metal band around the log's circumference at the desired tenon length. Using a square referenced against the longitudinal chalk line, they ensure the shoulder line is perfectly perpendicular to the log's axis. This line is deeply scribed with a marking knife, creating a physical groove for the saw to follow.
    3.  **Marking the Tenon Cheeks:** On the end grain of the log, the user marks the rectangle of the tenon. The lines for the tenon cheeks (the wide faces) are then extended along the log's surface from the end grain back to the scribed shoulder line using the chalk line.

### 2.2 Sawing the Tenon

#### 2.2.1 Cutting the Cheeks (Rip Cut)

*   **Physical Movements:**
    1.  The user stands to the side of the log, aligning their body with the marked cheek line.
    2.  They begin the cut by placing the saw on the far corner of the end grain and using their thumb as a guide to start the kerf. The first few strokes are short and controlled.
    3.  The cut starts at a 45-degree angle on the end grain. Once a shallow kerf is established, the saw is lowered to be horizontal, cutting along the chalk line towards the shoulder line.
    4.  The user saws with smooth, long strokes, using the full length of the blade. The focus is on keeping the saw blade perfectly vertical and stopping precisely at the scribed shoulder line. It is better to stop slightly proud of the line than to cut past it.
*   **Visual Appearance:**
    *   A thin, straight groove (the kerf) appears, gradually deepening.
    *   Sawdust will be coarse and stringy, as this is a rip cut (with the grain).
    *   The user frequently pauses to blow sawdust away and check that the cut is tracking on the lines on both the top surface and the end grain.

#### 2.2.2 Cutting the Shoulders (Crosscut)

*   **Physical Movements:**
    1.  The user repositions their body to be perpendicular to the log's length.
    2.  The crosscut saw is placed into the deeply scribed shoulder line. This initial scribe is vital as it prevents the saw from skittering across the curved surface of the log.
    3.  The user makes careful, deliberate cuts around the circumference of the log, rotating the log if necessary and possible, or changing their body position.
    4.  Each cut goes only as deep as the cheek saw cuts. The goal is to meet the cheek cuts precisely.
*   **Visual Appearance:**
    *   The saw kerf is wider than the rip cut and filled with finer, chip-like sawdust.
    *   As the cut deepens, a clean, sharp corner begins to form where the shoulder meets the tenon cheek. The large slabs of waste wood are now held on only by the remaining uncut portions.

### 2.3 Chiseling & Finishing

#### 2.3.1 Removing the Bulk Waste

*   **Physical Movements:**
    1.  The user takes a large framing chisel and a heavy mallet.
    2.  The chisel is placed in the shoulder cut, bevel facing the tenon, and angled slightly downwards.
    3.  With a few sharp mallet blows, a large chunk of the waste wood is split off. The user works their way around the tenon, breaking away the large blocks of wood freed by the saw cuts.
*   **Visual Appearance:**
    *   The waste wood splits away, often with a loud crack.
    *   The newly revealed surface of the tenon cheek is rough, uneven, and follows the grain of the wood. It is not a flat, smooth surface yet. It will have visible saw marks from the initial cheek cuts.

#### 2.3.2 Paring to the Line (Defining the Tenon)

*   **Physical Movements:**
    1.  The user switches to a wide, sharp chisel or a slick. No mallet is used for this stage.
    2.  The chisel is held with both hands, one on the handle for power and the other near the blade for guidance.
    3.  The user shaves thin layers of wood from the tenon cheeks, working down to the marked lines. The motion is a controlled push, using body weight for leverage.
    4.  For the shoulders, the user places the back of the chisel flat against the tenon cheek and pares vertically downwards to create a crisp, flat shoulder. This is done in small, controlled increments.
*   **Visual Appearance:**
    *   The rough, sawn surface of the tenon cheek becomes progressively smoother, showing long, shallow facets from the chisel blade. It will not be perfectly flat like a machined surface.
    *   The shoulder line becomes a sharp, well-defined 90-degree angle. There might be slight undercutting, which is acceptable, but no gaps.
    *   The final tenon has a hand-crafted look, with slight tool marks visible upon close inspection.

## 3.0 Mistakes to Avoid (Real vs. Fake)

| What Looks Fake (Common AI/CGI Mistakes) | What Looks Real (Mechanical Accuracy) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Perfectly Smooth Surfaces:** The tenon cheeks and shoulders are perfectly flat and smooth, as if sanded or milled. | **Visible Tool Marks:** The tenon cheeks have subtle, long facets from the chisel or slick. The shoulders are clean but may show a faint scribe line. |
| **Overshot Saw Cuts:** The shoulder saw cuts extend past the tenon cheeks into the final piece. | **Precise Stops:** The saw cuts stop just shy of or exactly at the intersecting line. Any cleanup is done with a chisel. A slight amount of wood might be chiseled from the corner to ensure the joint closes. |
| **No Scribe Lines:** The user just starts cutting on the surface. | **Deeply Scribed Lines:** A visible knife or scribe line is established first, especially for the shoulder. The saw blade sits in this line to start the cut. |
| **Effortless Sawing/Chopping:** The user saws through the log with no resistance. Large chunks are removed with a single, light tap. | **Realistic Effort:** Sawing requires a steady rhythm and visible effort. Chiseling large waste requires forceful mallet strikes that make a solid "thud" sound. Paring requires controlled, focused pressure. |
| **Instant Waste Removal:** The large waste blocks simply disappear after the saw cuts are made. | **Splitting Out Waste:** The waste is deliberately split out with a chisel and mallet after being freed by the saw. It breaks along the grain, not in a clean, geometric shape. |
| **Perfectly Square Shoulders on a Round Log:** The transition from the round log to the flat shoulder is an impossibly sharp, perfect intersection. | **Gradual Transition:** While the shoulder itself is flat, the transition from the round log surface will be hand-carved and may show slight inconsistencies. The shoulder is squared from the tenon face itself, not the exterior of the log. |
| **Loose, Gappy Fit:** The finished tenon has visible gaps at the shoulders before being assembled. | **A Tight, Push Fit:** A well-made joint should require a few light taps with a mallet to seat fully. There should be no visible gaps at the shoulders. |
