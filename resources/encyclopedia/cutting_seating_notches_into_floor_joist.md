# Technical Guide for AI Video Generation: Cutting Seating Notches in Floor Joists

This guide provides a detailed, step-by-step breakdown of the physical process of cutting seating notches, commonly known as "birdsmouth" joints, into floor joists. The focus is on achieving a high degree of realism and mechanical accuracy in the generated video content.

## 1.0 Tools and Materials

### 1.1 Required Tools
*   **Circular Saw:** A standard 7 1/4-inch saw is typical.
*   **Hand Saw or Jigsaw:** For finishing cuts where the circular saw cannot reach.
*   **Chisel:** A sharp, one-inch wide wood chisel is ideal.
*   **Hammer or Mallet:** To use with the chisel.
*   **Speed Square:** Essential for marking accurate angles.
*   **Measuring Tape:** For precise measurements.
*   **Carpenter's Pencil:** For clear, visible markings.
*   **Safety Glasses:** A crucial piece of personal protective equipment.

### 1.2 Materials
*   **Floor Joist:** Typically dimensional lumber (e.g., 2x8, 2x10). The wood should be new and without major defects.
*   **Support Pillar/Beam:** The surface against which the joist will sit.

## 2.0 Step-by-Step Process

### 2.1 Marking the Notch

**Physical Cues:**
*   The carpenter stands alongside the joist, which is typically resting on sawhorses.
*   The body is stable, with feet shoulder-width apart.
*   The non-dominant hand holds the speed square firmly against the edge of the joist.
*   The dominant hand holds the carpenter's pencil.

**Visual Appearance:**
*   The pencil lines should be sharp and clear against the grain of the wood.
*   The "birdsmouth" is a triangular notch composed of two cuts: the horizontal "seat cut" that rests on the support, and the vertical "heel cut" that abuts the side of the support.

**Step-by-Step Movements:**
1.  **Measure and Mark the Seat Cut:**
    *   The carpenter uses the measuring tape to determine the length of the seat cut, which corresponds to the width of the support pillar.
    *   A small, precise mark is made with the pencil.
2.  **Mark the Heel Cut Angle:**
    *   The speed square is placed on the edge of the joist with the pivot point aligned with the end of the seat cut mark.
    *   The square is rotated to the desired roof pitch or angle.
    *   A line is drawn along the edge of the speed square to mark the heel cut.
3.  **Mark the Seat Cut Line:**
    *   The speed square is then turned to a 90-degree angle to the heel cut line.
    *   A line is drawn to complete the triangular outline of the notch.

### 2.2 Making the Initial Cuts with a Circular Saw

**Physical Cues:**
*   The carpenter has a firm, two-handed grip on the circular saw.
*   The body is positioned to the side of the cut line, never directly behind the saw.
*   The eyes are focused on the blade guide, aligning it with the pencil mark.

**Visual Appearance:**
*   The saw blade spins rapidly, creating a slight blur.
*   Sawdust is ejected from the side of the saw, away from the user.
*   As the blade cuts, the wood fibers are cleanly severed.

**Step-by-Step Movements:**
1.  **Set the Blade Depth:**
    *   The carpenter unplugs the saw and adjusts the blade depth to match the deepest part of the notch.
2.  **Cut the Heel Cut:**
    *   The saw is brought up to full speed before it contacts the wood.
    *   The cut is made in a smooth, steady motion, following the pencil line. The cut should stop precisely at the corner of the notch to avoid overcutting.
3.  **Cut the Seat Cut:**
    *   The same process is repeated for the seat cut, again stopping at the corner.

### 2.3 Removing the Waste with a Chisel

**Physical Cues:**
*   The carpenter holds the chisel with the dominant hand, with the bevel side facing the waste wood.
*   The non-dominant hand cups the blade for stability and control, with fingers kept behind the cutting edge.
*   The body is braced, and the force comes from controlled taps with the hammer, not wild swings.

**Visual Appearance:**
*   The chisel should be sharp, with a clean, reflective edge.
*   With each tap of the hammer, a chip of wood is removed.
*   The final surface of the notch should be smooth and flat.

**Step-by-Step Movements:**
1.  **Initial Waste Removal:**
    *   The chisel is placed vertically at the corner of the notch, and a firm tap with the hammer removes the bulk of the waste wood.
2.  **Paring and Smoothing:**
    *   The chisel is then used to pare away the remaining high spots.
    *   The angle of the chisel is low, and the cuts are made with a slicing motion.
    *   The final passes are very thin to create a perfectly flat and smooth surface.

### 2.4 Test Fitting the Joist

**Physical Cues:**
*   The carpenter lifts the joist with both hands, using their legs for leverage.
*   The joist is carefully lowered onto the support pillar.
*   The carpenter may wiggle the joist slightly to check for a snug fit.

**Visual Appearance:**
*   A perfectly cut notch will sit flush against the support pillar with no visible gaps.
*   The joist should not rock or move when seated.

## 3.0 Mistakes to Avoid (Fake vs. Real)

### 3.1 Overcutting with the Circular Saw
*   **What Looks Fake:** A saw cut that extends beyond the corner of the notch. This is a common amateur mistake that weakens the joist.
*   **What Looks Real:** The saw cuts for the heel and seat meet precisely at the corner of the notch.

### 3.2 A Rough, Uneven Notch Surface
*   **What Looks Fake:** A notch with a rough, splintered, or uneven surface. This indicates a dull chisel or improper technique.
*   **What Looks Real:** The inside surfaces of the notch are smooth and flat, showing the marks of a sharp chisel.

### 3.3 Gaps and a Loose Fit
*   **What Looks Fake:** Visible gaps between the joist and the support pillar when the notch is seated. The joist appears wobbly.
*   **What Looks Real:** The joist sits tightly and securely on the support, with the heel and seat cuts making full contact.

### 3.4 Tear-Out and Splintering
*   **What Looks Fake:** The edges of the cut are jagged and splintered. This can be caused by a dull saw blade or cutting too quickly.
*   **What Looks Real:** The edges of the cut are clean and sharp, with minimal to no splintering of the wood fibers. Using painter's tape along the cut line before sawing can help achieve this.
