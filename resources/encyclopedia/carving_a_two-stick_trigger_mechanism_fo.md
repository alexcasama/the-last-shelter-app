# Technical Guide: Carving a Two-Stick Snare Trap Trigger

This guide provides a detailed, step-by-step technical breakdown for generating a realistic video of a survivalist carving a two-stick trigger mechanism for a snare trap. The focus is on mechanical accuracy, physical realism, and the subtle details that differentiate an authentic depiction from a fabricated one. This document will focus on the "Figure-Four" style trigger, a common and effective mechanism.

## 1.0 Materials and Tools

For a realistic depiction, the materials and tools should be appropriate for a survival scenario.

### 1.1 Wood Selection
*   **Visual Appearance:** The sticks should be sourced from dead, dry, and seasoned hardwood if possible. Green wood can be used but may be less reliable as it dries and changes shape. The wood should be free of knots and cracks, which would be weak points. The diameter of the sticks should be roughly the thickness of a thumb or a Sharpie marker.
*   **Physical Cues:** The survivalist would test the wood's strength by attempting to bend it. The sound should be a dry snap rather than a green, fibrous tear if a piece is broken.

### 1.2 Carving Tool
*   **Visual Appearance:** A high-carbon steel knife with a single bevel (a "Scandi grind") is a common and realistic choice for bushcraft. The blade should be between 4-6 inches long. The handle should be simple and ergonomic, made of wood or another natural material. Avoid pristine, factory-new knives. A realistic knife would show signs of use: a patina on the blade, minor scratches, and a handle worn smooth from use.
*   **Physical Cues:** The knife should be visibly sharp. This can be demonstrated by the ease with which it shaves thin curls of wood.

## 2.0 Body Mechanics and Posture

Safety and control are paramount when carving. A realistic depiction will show the survivalist in a stable and safe position.

*   **Seated Position:** The most common and stable position is seated, with elbows resting on the knees. This creates a "blood circle," an imaginary bubble of safety around the carver. Any carving motions should be directed away from the body and legs.
*   **Bracing the Workpiece:** The stick being carved should be braced against the thigh or held firmly with the non-dominant hand, well behind the path of the blade. For more powerful cuts, the stick can be braced against a log or the ground.

## 3.0 Knife Grips and Carving Motions

The way the knife is held is critical for both realism and depicting control.

*   **Forehand Grip:** This is a basic, powerful grip, like holding a hammer. It's used for rough shaping and removing larger amounts of wood. The motion is a controlled push or "punch" away from the body, using the shoulder and elbow rather than just the wrist.
*   **Chest-Lever Grip:** A powerful and controlled grip where the knife handle is held palm-up, with the blade facing away from the body. The knife is drawn towards the chest in a levering motion, with the arms kept tight to the body for control. This is used for making long, controlled cuts.
*   **Thumb-Assisted Push Cut:** For fine, detailed work, the thumb of the non-dominant hand is placed on the spine of the blade to provide extra pressure and control. This allows for precise removal of small shavings of wood.

## 4.0 Step-by-Step Carving Process: The Figure-Four Trigger

The Figure-Four trigger consists of three pieces: the vertical post, the diagonal lever, and the horizontal trigger/bait stick.

### 4.1 Carving the Vertical Post

This piece stands upright and holds the other two components.

*   **Step 1: Squaring the End:** The bottom of the stick is carved flat so it can sit securely on the ground without being driven into the soil like a stake.
    *   **Visual Cues:** Small, deliberate slicing motions are used to create a flat, 90-degree surface. The freshly cut wood will be lighter in color than the bark.
*   **Step 2: Carving the Chisel Point:** The top of the stick is carved into a "chisel" point, resembling a flathead screwdriver. This is done by carving two opposing flat faces that meet at a sharp edge.
    *   **Physical Cues:** The carver will likely use a thumb-assisted push cut for this, carefully removing thin layers of wood to create the flat planes.
*   **Step 3: Carving the Facets:** This is a critical and often fumbled step. Two flat facets are carved into the side of the post. One is on the same plane as the chisel top, and the other is at a right angle to it. This creates a square edge that will hold the horizontal trigger stick.
    *   **Visual Cues:** The facets should be clean and flat, not rounded. The intersection of the two facets should form a sharp, 90-degree angle.

### 4.2 Carving the Diagonal Lever

This piece sits on top of the vertical post and holds the weight of the deadfall.

*   **Step 1: Carving the Notch:** A notch is carved into the side of the stick, near one end. This notch will sit on the chisel point of the vertical post.
    *   **Physical Cues:** To make the notch, a "stop cut" is first made by pressing the knife straight down into the wood. Then, a shaving of wood is removed by carving towards the stop cut.
*   **Step 2: Carving the Chisel Point:** The other end of the diagonal lever is carved into a chisel point, similar to the one on the vertical post. This will lock into the horizontal trigger stick.

### 4.3 Carving the Horizontal Trigger/Bait Stick

This is the most complex piece, as it connects the other two and holds the bait.

*   **Step 1: Carving the Side Notch:** A notch is carved into the side of the stick, which will engage with the square edge on the vertical post.
*   **Step 2: Carving the End Notch:** A notch is carved near the end of the stick, but at a 90-degree rotation from the side notch. This notch will receive the chisel point of the diagonal lever.
    *   **Visual Cues:** The two notches on this stick must be perpendicular to each other for the trap to set correctly.
*   **Step 3: Pointing the Bait End:** The other end of the stick is sharpened to a point to skewer the bait.

## 5.0 Mistakes to Avoid for Realism

*   **Overly Smooth Surfaces:** Hand-carved wood will have facets and slight imperfections. Avoid surfaces that look sanded or machine-cut.
*   **Incorrect Notches:** Rounded or poorly defined notches will not hold under tension and are a sign of an unrealistic depiction. The edges should be crisp and the angles precise.
*   **Ignoring Wood Grain:** A realistic carving will follow the grain of the wood. Carving against the grain can cause the wood to tear out and split.
*   **Unsafe Practices:** The AI should not depict the carver cutting towards themselves without proper bracing, or with their hands in the path of the blade.
*   **Perfectly Proportioned Sticks:** In a survival situation, the sticks will be what is available. They may be slightly bent or have minor blemishes. Perfection is unrealistic.
*   **Friction Points:** Many guides get it wrong by showing trigger contact points as perfectly flat, which increases friction. For a more sensitive and realistic trap, these points can be slightly rounded to reduce surface area.
*   **Trap Angle:** A common mistake is setting the deadfall at too high an angle. A realistic and effective set will have the deadfall at a 30 to 45-degree angle. This gives the animal less time to escape.
