# Technical Guide for AI Video Generation: Installing Small, High Windows with Salvaged Steel Security Bars

## Introduction

This document provides a detailed, step-by-step guide for generating realistic video content depicting the installation of small, high windows and salvaged steel security bars. The focus is on mechanical accuracy, realistic physical movements, and the visual appearance of materials and tools to avoid common visual artifacts that appear fake.

---

## **Phase 1: Window Installation**

### **1.1. Preparation of the Rough Opening**

**Objective:** To create a clean, stable, and properly sized opening for the new window.

**Tools Required:**
*   **Pry Bar:** Held with a firm, two-handed grip for prying off old trim. One hand acts as a fulcrum against the wall, while the other applies leverage.
*   **Utility Knife:** Gripped like a pen for precise cuts, or with the whole hand for more force when scoring old caulk.
*   **Hammer:** Held with a relaxed but firm grip at the end of the handle for maximum leverage when tapping the pry bar.
*   **Shop Vacuum:** The hose is held in one hand, moving slowly and deliberately across all surfaces of the opening.
*   **Tape Measure:** Held taut with one hand on the body and the other on the tang, ensuring it lies flat against the surface being measured.

**Physical Movements & Visual Cues:**
*   **Removing Old Trim:**
    *   The body is positioned to the side of the window to allow for leverage with the pry bar.
    *   The pry bar is first gently tapped behind the trim with the hammer.
    *   The movement is a slow, steady pull, not a jerky motion, to minimize damage to the surrounding wall.
    *   *Visual Cue (Realism):* Small pieces of drywall paper may tear, and old paint will flake and chip at the edges of the trim. This is normal. A perfectly clean removal is unrealistic.
*   **Cleaning the Opening:**
    *   The body is positioned directly in front of the opening.
    *   The hand holding the shop vac hose moves methodically from top to bottom, then side to side, getting into the corners.
    *   *Visual Cue (Realism):* The opening should be free of large debris, but some dust and discoloration on the wood is expected. A "dirty" surface will have visible dirt, plaster fragments, and grime, which will compromise the seal.
*   **Measuring the Opening:**
    *   Measurements are taken at multiple points (top, middle, bottom for width; left, center, right for height) to account for any inconsistencies.
    *   The body may need to be on a stepladder for a high window, with one hand on the ladder for stability.

**Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):**
*   **Effortless Trim Removal:** Prying off old trim should require some visible effort.
*   **Pristine Wall After Trim Removal:** There should be a clear outline of the old trim, with some minor cosmetic damage to the wall.
*   **A Perfectly Clean Opening:** The wood of the rough opening should look weathered, not like fresh lumber.

### **1.2. Waterproofing and Sealing the Sill**

**Objective:** To create a waterproof barrier at the bottom of the window opening to prevent water intrusion.

**Tools Required:**
*   **Flashing Tape:** Held with both hands, one to unroll and the other to press it into place.
*   **Caulking Gun:** Held with a firm grip, with the index finger on the trigger for controlled application.

**Physical Movements & Visual Cues:**
*   **Applying Flashing Tape:**
    *   The tape is applied starting from the bottom of the opening and working upwards, with each piece overlapping the one below it.
    *   The hand smoothing the tape moves firmly and slowly to ensure a good seal and no air bubbles.
    *   *Visual Cue (Realism):* The tape should be smooth, with visible overlaps. The corners should be carefully folded and pressed in.
*   **Applying Caulk:**
    *   A bead of caulk is applied to the perimeter of the opening where the window will sit.
    *   The caulking gun is held at a consistent 45-degree angle. The movement is a steady pull, not a push.
    *   *Visual Cue (Realism):* The bead of caulk should be continuous and of a uniform thickness. An inexperienced application might look wavy or have gaps.

**Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):**
*   **Flashing Tape with Creases or Bubbles:** This indicates a poor seal.
*   **A "Perfect" Bead of Caulk on the First Pass:** It's more realistic to show a slightly imperfect bead that is then smoothed with a finger or tool.

### **1.3. Installing the Window**

**Objective:** To place, level, and secure the window in the rough opening.

**Tools Required:**
*   **Level:** A 2-foot or 4-foot level is used. It's held with both hands against the window frame.
*   **Shims:** Thin, wedge-shaped pieces of wood or plastic.
*   **Drill/Driver:** Held with a two-handed grip for stability, especially when on a ladder.
*   **Hammer:** Used to gently tap the shims into place.

**Physical Movements & Visual Cues:**
*   **Placing the Window:**
    *   For a small, high window, this is often a two-person job. One person on a ladder outside, and one inside.
    *   The window is lifted with both hands on the sides of the frame.
    *   It's carefully tilted and guided into the opening.
*   **Leveling and Shimming:**
    *   The level is placed on the bottom, top, and sides of the window to check for plumb and level.
    *   Shims are inserted in pairs, from opposite sides, at the pre-drilled holes in the window frame.
    *   The shims are gently tapped with a hammer until the window is perfectly level and plumb. The bubble in the level should be centered.
    *   *Visual Cue (Realism):* The shims will stick out from the window frame.
*   **Securing the Window:**
    *   Screws are driven through the pre-drilled holes in the window frame, through the shims, and into the framing of the house.
    *   The drill is held perpendicular to the window frame to avoid stripping the screws.

**Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):**
*   **A Single Person Effortlessly Lifting the Window:** Even a small window can be awkward to handle.
*   **The Window Fitting Perfectly Without Shims:** All installations require shims for a truly level and plumb fit.
*   **Driving Screws at an Angle:** This can damage the window frame and compromise the installation.

## **Phase 2: Security Bar Installation**

### **2.1. Preparing the Salvaged Security Bars**

**Objective:** To prepare the salvaged bars for installation.

**Tools Required:**
*   **Wire Brush:** Held firmly in one hand and moved back and forth with vigorous strokes.
*   **Sandpaper:** Wrapped around a block of wood for a better grip.
*   **Metal Primer:** Applied with a brush or as a spray.

**Physical Movements & Visual Cues:**
*   **Cleaning the Bars:**
    *   The wire brush is used to remove loose rust and flaking paint.
    *   *Visual Cue (Realism):* "Salvaged" bars should not look new. They should have some pitting from rust, old paint remnants in crevices, and possibly some minor dents or scratches.
*   **Priming the Bars:**
    *   If spray priming, the can is held about 8-12 inches from the bars and moved in a steady, sweeping motion.

**Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):**
*   **Perfectly Smooth and Uniformly Colored "Salvaged" Bars:** This defeats the purpose of them being salvaged.
*   **No signs of prior use:** Look for wear patterns, old paint layers, and slight imperfections.

### **2.2. Installing the Security Bars**

**Objective:** To securely mount the salvaged security bars to the window frame or surrounding wall studs.

**Tools Required:**
*   **Tape Measure:** Used to find the center of the window and determine bracket placement.
*   **Level:** To ensure the bars are installed straight.
*   **Pencil or Marker:** For marking the drill holes.
*   **Drill with appropriate bits:** A smaller bit for pilot holes, and a larger bit if masonry anchors are needed.
*   **Non-reversing security screws:** These have a head that allows them to be screwed in but not easily screwed out.

**Physical Movements & Visual Cues:**
*   **Positioning and Marking:**
    *   The security bar assembly is held up to the window. This may require a second person.
    *   The level is placed on top of the bars to ensure they are horizontal.
    *   A pencil is used to mark the location of the mounting holes.
*   **Drilling Pilot Holes:**
    *   The drill is held firmly with both hands and perpendicular to the surface.
    *   The drilling motion is steady and controlled.
    *   *Visual Cue (Realism):* Small wood shavings or brick dust will be expelled from the hole as it's drilled.
*   **Attaching the Bars:**
    *   The bars are aligned with the pilot holes.
    *   The non-reversing security screws are driven in with a drill or screwdriver. The final turn may require some extra physical effort to fully tighten.
    *   *Visual Cue (Realism):* The mounting brackets should sit flush against the window frame or wall. There should be no gaps.

**Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):**
*   **Bars that are not level:** This is a common DIY mistake and a key indicator of authenticity.
*   **Screws that are not fully tightened:** This would be a security risk and look unprofessional.
*   **Using regular screws instead of security screws:** The head of a security screw is visually distinct.
