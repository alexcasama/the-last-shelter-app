# Technical Guide: Constructing and Installing Angled Roof Rafters on a Log Structure

## 1.0 Introduction

This guide provides a detailed, step-by-step technical breakdown for constructing and installing angled roof rafters on a log structure. The focus is on mechanical accuracy, realistic physical movements, and visual details to ensure an authentic representation.

### 1.1 Core Principles of Realism
*   **Embrace Imperfection:** Log structures are crafted from natural, irregular materials. Rafters will not be perfectly uniform. The fitting process involves scribing and custom cutting for each location.
*   **Gravity and Weight:** Rafters, especially those made from whole logs or large timbers, are heavy. Movements should reflect the strain and effort of lifting, positioning, and securing these elements.
*   **Tool Marks:** The final appearance of the wood should show subtle, realistic marks from the tools used (e.g., chainsaw cuts, adze marks, planer smoothing).

## 2.0 Tools and Materials

### 2.1 Required Tools & Proper Handling

*   **Chainsaw:**
    *   **Holding:** Held with a firm, two-handed grip. The front hand wraps around the handlebar, thumb curled under, and the rear hand controls the throttle. The body is positioned to the side of the cutting line, never directly behind the saw.
    *   **Visual Cue:** The operator's stance is wide and stable. Sawdust should be visibly ejecting away from the user.
*   **Speed Square (Rafter Square):**
    *   **Holding:** Held flat against the timber with the fence (the lipped edge) pressed firmly against the edge of the rafter. The fingers of one hand hold it steady while the other hand uses a pencil to mark along the edge.
    *   **Visual Cue:** The square's markings for angles and pitches are clearly visible. The pencil line should be crisp and dark.
*   **Tape Measure:**
    *   **Holding:** The body of the tape measure is held in one hand, while the other hand hooks the metal tang onto the end of the log or board. The tape is pulled taut to get an accurate measurement.
    *   **Visual Cue:** The tape should not sag. The user often has to bend or crouch to read the measurement at eye level.
*   **Log Scribe:**
    *   **Holding:** Held delicately, like a bird, to allow it to follow the contours of the lower log accurately. The bubbles in the built-in levels must be centered to ensure a true vertical transfer of the profile.
    *   **Visual Cue:** The user moves slowly and deliberately along the log, keeping their eyes fixed on the levels and the pencil tip.
*   **Electric Hand Planer:**
    *   **Holding:** Gripped with both hands, one on the front knob to guide it and the other on the main handle to control the power and forward motion.
    *   **Visual Cue:** Wood shavings should be seen ejecting from the side of the planer. The user moves the planer in smooth, overlapping passes.
*   **Drill (Heavy-Duty 1/2"):**
    *   **Holding:** A two-handed grip is essential, especially with large auger bits. One hand is on the pistol grip, and the other is on the auxiliary handle to counteract the torque.
    *   **Visual Cue:** The user's body should be braced to control the drill's power. Wood chips will be expelled from the hole as the bit advances.
*   **Sledgehammer & Wooden Pegs/Rebar:**
    *   **Holding:** The sledgehammer is swung with a full-body motion, using the legs and core for power, not just the arms. One hand is near the base of the handle, the other near the head for guidance, sliding down as the swing is made.
    *   **Visual Cue:** The impact should be solid, driving the peg or rebar with a distinct "thud." The wood around the peg may slightly compress.

### 2.2 Materials

*   **Rafter Logs/Timbers:** These can be round logs, often with a flattened top surface for the roof sheathing, or squared-off timbers. They should have their bark removed. The wood may have checks (cracks) and knots, which is natural.
*   **Ridge Pole:** A large-diameter log that forms the peak of the roof.
*   **Wall Logs (Top Plate):** The uppermost logs of the cabin walls where the rafters will rest.
*   **Fasteners:** Large structural screws (e.g., TimberLOKs), or wooden pegs (dowels) made from a hardwood like oak. Rebar may also be used to pin the rafters in place.

## 3.0 Step-by-Step Construction and Installation

### Step 1: Preparing the Rafters

1.  **Flattening the Top Surface (if using round logs):**
    *   **Physical Movement:** The log is secured on sawhorses. The user snaps a chalk line down the length of the log to mark the edges of the flat plane. An electric hand planer or an adze is used to remove the wood between the lines. The user moves steadily along the length of the log.
    *   **Visual Appearance:** The top of the log transitions from a curved, peeled surface to a flat, relatively smooth plane. There will be visible tool marks and small imperfections.

### Step 2: Marking the Rafter Cuts

This is the most critical phase for achieving the correct angle and a snug fit.

1.  **Marking the Plumb Cut (Ridge Pole Connection):**
    *   **Physical Movement:** Place the rafter on sawhorses. The user holds a speed square near one end of the rafter. They pivot the square until the desired roof pitch (e.g., '12' on the "Common" scale for a 12/12 pitch) aligns with the edge of the rafter. Holding the square firmly, a pencil is drawn along the straight edge of the square to mark the angled line.
    *   **Visual Appearance:** A sharp, angled pencil line is now visible across the top flat surface and down the sides of the rafter.

2.  **Marking the Birdsmouth Cut (Wall Connection):**
    *   **Physical Movement:** Measure the total length of the rafter from the long point of the plumb cut. At this mark, the speed square is again used to draw a line at the same angle as the plumb cut; this is the "shoulder cut." To mark the "seat cut" (the horizontal part), the square is positioned perpendicular to the shoulder cut line. The user must measure and mark the depth of the seat cut to match the width of the wall log it will sit on.
    *   **Visual Appearance:** The two pencil lines form a triangular notch on the underside of the rafter. This is the "birdsmouth."

### Step 3: Cutting the Rafters

1.  **Executing the Cuts:**
    *   **Physical Movement:** The user grips the chainsaw firmly. For the plumb cut and the birdsmouth cuts, the saw is carefully guided along the pencil lines. The cuts are often finished with a handsaw for precision. The user's body is stable, and their eyes are focused on the line.
    *   **Visual Appearance:** The end of the rafter now has a clean, angled cut. The birdsmouth notch is removed, leaving a seat for the wall log. The cut surface will have the distinct, slightly rough texture of a chainsaw cut.

### Step 4: Scribing the Rafter to the Wall Log

This step is crucial for a tight fit on an irregular round log wall.

1.  **Temporary Placement:** The cut rafter is temporarily hoisted into position, resting on the wall log.
2.  **Scribing the Fit:**
    *   **Physical Movement:** The user holds the log scribe with the two points vertically aligned (checking the bubble levels). The bottom point of the scribe is traced along the contour of the wall log, while the top point (with the pencil) simultaneously transfers that exact profile onto the side of the rafter's birdsmouth. This is a slow, careful movement.
    *   **Visual Appearance:** A wavy, irregular pencil line is now visible inside the birdsmouth cut, perfectly mirroring the shape of the log below it.

3.  **Custom Fitting:**
    *   **Physical Movement:** The rafter is taken down. Using a chainsaw, grinder, or chisel, the user carefully removes the wood up to the scribed line. This requires precise control of the tool.
    *   **Visual Appearance:** The flat seat of the birdsmouth is now custom-contoured to match the wall log.

### Step 5: Installation and Securing

1.  **Hoisting the Rafters:**
    *   **Physical Movement:** This is a team effort. Two or more people, using ropes or manual lifting, hoist the rafter into its final position. The movements are coordinated and strenuous. The rafter is first seated against the ridge pole, then lowered onto the wall log.
    *   **Visual Appearance:** The rafter is maneuvered into place. The plumb cut should sit flush against the ridge pole, and the custom-scribed birdsmouth should fit snugly onto the wall log with minimal gaps.

2.  **Securing the Rafters:**
    *   **Physical Movement (Drilling):** The user braces themselves and drills a hole through the rafter and deep into the ridge pole at an angle. Another hole is drilled down through the birdsmouth into the wall log.
    *   **Physical Movement (Fastening):** A sledgehammer is used to drive a long wooden peg or a piece of rebar into the drilled holes. The swings are powerful and rhythmic. If using structural screws, the heavy-duty drill drives them in with significant torque.
    *   **Visual Appearance:** The head of the peg or rebar is now visible, flush or slightly countersunk into the rafter. The rafter is now solidly locked into place.

## 4.0 Mistakes to Avoid (Fake vs. Real)

| What Looks Fake (Mistakes) | What Looks Real (Authentic) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Perfectly Uniform Rafters:** All rafters are identical in size and shape, like dimensional lumber. | **Natural Variation:** Rafters have slight differences in diameter, straightness, and surface texture. Each one is unique. |
| **Floating Rafters:** Large, visible gaps between the rafter's birdsmouth and the round wall log. | **Scribed Fit:** The rafter's notch is custom-cut to the contour of the wall log, resulting in a very tight, almost seamless fit. |
| **No Overhangs:** The rafters end flush with the walls. | **Generous Overhangs:** Roof overhangs should be significant (24-30 inches on the sides, 4-6 feet on the ends) to protect the logs from moisture. |
| **Clean, Factory-Perfect Connections:** All connections are made with modern metal brackets (like hurricane ties) that look out of place. | **Traditional Joinery:** Connections are secured with less-visible methods like large structural screws, or more traditional wooden pegs and rebar. |
| **Effortless Lifting:** A single person easily lifts and places a large log rafter. | **Realistic Effort:** Hoisting rafters is shown as a difficult, team-based activity, reflecting the true weight of the materials. |
| **Logs Too Close to the Ground:** The bottom course of wall logs is near the soil. | **Proper Elevation:** The first course of logs is at least 1.5 to 2 feet off the ground to prevent splash-back and rot. |
| **Exposed End Grain:** The ends of the rafters and ridge pole are left exposed past the fascia line. | **Protected End Grain:** The roof structure and fascia protect the end grain of the structural logs from direct exposure to weather, which prevents water from wicking into the logs. |
