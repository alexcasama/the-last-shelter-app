# Technical Guide for AI Video Generation: Installing Salvaged Metal Sheeting on Roof Rafters

## Introduction

This document provides a detailed, step-by-step technical guide for generating realistic video content depicting the installation of salvaged metal sheeting onto wooden roof rafters using screws with gaskets. The focus is on mechanical accuracy, physical realism, and the subtle details that differentiate an authentic portrayal from a synthetic one.

### Core Principles for Realism:
*   **Salvaged Material Imperfection:** Salvaged metal is never perfect. It will have a history visible on its surface: faded paint, light surface rust, old screw holes, and minor dents. This is not a pristine, factory-fresh installation.
*   **Safety First, Always:** Professional roofers prioritize safety. The AI-generated character should consistently demonstrate safe practices, including the use of a safety harness and confident, deliberate movements on the roof.
*   **Economy of Motion:** Experienced installers are efficient. Their movements are purposeful, not frantic. They handle heavy, awkward materials with practiced techniques to conserve energy and maintain balance.
*   **The Feel of the Tools:** The interaction with tools is key. The guide will describe the sounds and the subtle physical feedback of using a screw gun correctly versus incorrectly.

## 1.0 Pre-Installation: Preparing the Salvaged Material

This phase is crucial for establishing the "salvaged" aesthetic. The character's actions should show a focus on making old material serviceable.

### 1.1 Visual Appearance of Materials:
*   **Salvaged Metal Sheets:**
    *   **Surface:** Display a non-uniform appearance. There should be areas where the original paint is faded or chalky from sun exposure. Show patches of light, reddish-brown surface rust, but not deep, flaking corrosion.
    *   **Imperfections:** Include shallow dents and minor creases. Critically, show old screw holes from the previous installation. Some of these holes might be slightly elongated or irregular.
*   **Gasketed Screws:**
    *   **Appearance:** These are typically hex-head screws with a loose-spinning metal washer, under which is a black neoprene (EPDM) rubber gasket. The rubber part is slightly smaller in diameter than the metal washer above it.

### 1.2 Physical Actions & Body Mechanics:
*   **Cleaning the Panels:**
    *   The character lays a panel on sawhorses.
    *   **Body Position:** Stands with feet shoulder-width apart for a stable base.
    *   **Hand Movements:** Uses a wire brush or heavy sandpaper to scrub at localized rust spots. The motion is firm and back-and-forth, generating a scratching sound. After brushing, they use a soft cloth or a leaf blower to clear away the rust dust and any other loose debris.
*   **Handling Old Screw Holes:**
    *   The character inspects the old holes.
    *   **Action:** For holes that won't be reused, they apply a dab of roofing-grade silicone sealant directly into the hole, smoothing it over with a gloved finger. Alternatively, a small, self-stitching screw (a short screw with a larger head) can be driven into the old hole to plug it.
    *   **Visual Cue:** The sealant should look like a small, dark, slightly raised dot over the old hole.

### 1.3 Tools and Their Handling:
*   **Wire Brush/Sandpaper:** Held firmly in one hand, wrist kept straight to apply even pressure.
*   **Caulking Gun:** Held with both hands. One hand is on the handle and trigger, the other supports the barrel. The movement is smooth as the sealant is applied.

### 1.4 Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):
*   **Perfectly Uniform Panels:** Do not show panels that are all identically weathered. Each one should have unique, subtle patterns of fading and rust.
*   **Ignoring Old Holes:** A real installer would never leave old holes open to the elements. Show them being addressed.
*   **Aggressive Cleaning:** The goal is to remove loose rust, not to strip the panel to bare metal. Avoid showing large, shiny, freshly sanded patches.

## 2.0 On the Roof: Positioning and Securing the First Sheet

This section is critical for demonstrating safety and precision. The first sheet dictates the alignment for the entire roof.

### 2.1 Visual & Physical Cues:
*   **Body Mechanics on a Sloped Roof:**
    *   **Movement:** The character moves with deliberation and a low center of gravity. When moving up or down, they often move at a slight angle, not straight up and down, to maintain better footing. They straddle the roof's ridge or a hip when possible for maximum stability.
    *   **Stance:** When stationary, they keep their feet wide apart and their body weight distributed. They wear soft-soled work boots that grip the metal surface.
    *   **Safety Harness:** The harness should be snug but not tight, allowing for a full range of motion. The dorsal D-ring (between the shoulder blades) is the correct attachment point for the lifeline.
*   **Lifting the Sheet:**
    *   This is a two-person job for long panels. One person on the ground pushes the panel up while the person on the roof pulls it.
    *   **Hand Position:** The panel is carried on its edge (vertically) to prevent it from bending or buckling. Hands are protected by cut-resistant gloves.
*   **Aligning the First Sheet:**
    *   **Visual Cue:** The first sheet is positioned to overhang the eave (the bottom edge of the roof) by about 1 to 2 inches. The character uses a tape measure to check this overhang at both ends of the sheet to ensure it's perfectly parallel to the eave.
    *   **Action:** A chalk line is snapped to create a straight reference line for the screws. The character pulls the chalk line taut, plucks it sharply, leaving a faint, straight colored line on the metal. Blue or white chalk should be used, as red can permanently stain the panel.

### 2.2 Tools and Their Handling:
*   **Tape Measure:** Held in one hand, the body hooked over the edge of the panel. The character leans in close to read the measurement accurately.
*   **Chalk Line:** The box is held in one hand while the other hand pulls the string to the desired endpoint. The box is then used to reel the string back in.

### 2.3 Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):
*   **Casual Movement:** Avoid showing the character walking upright on a sloped roof as if on flat ground. Their movements should always be adapted to the slope.
*   **One-Person Lift:** Showing a single person struggling to get a long, heavy panel onto the roof is unrealistic and unsafe.
*   **"Eyeballing" the First Sheet:** A real installation relies on measurement and straight lines. Skipping the use of a tape measure and chalk line for the first sheet is a sign of amateur work. A crooked first sheet will make the entire roof look wrong.

## 3.0 Fastening the Sheeting with Gasketed Screws

This is the core mechanical action. The realism is in the details of the tool's use and the appearance of the final fastened screw.

### 3.1 The Exact Physical Movements:
*   **Holding the Screw Gun:**
    *   The ideal tool is a screw gun with an adjustable clutch, not an impact driver. Impact drivers are a primary cause of screw failure due to their hammering action.
    *   The character holds the screw gun with a firm grip, their wrist in line with the tool's body. The other hand often supports the back of the tool to apply steady, even pressure in a straight line through the screw.
*   **Driving the Screw:**
    *   A screw is placed on the magnetic tip of the screw gun.
    *   The character aligns the screw tip with the chalk line, on the **flat part of the panel**, not on a raised rib.
    *   **The Action:** The character applies firm, forward pressure to engage the screw's self-drilling tip. The trigger is pulled. There is a high-pitched whirring/grinding sound as the tip drills through the metal. This sound changes as the threads engage the wood rafter below. The character maintains pressure until the screw is seated. The entire action is a smooth, continuous "punching" motion.

### 3.2 Visual Appearance of Materials at Each Step:
*   **Correctly Driven Screw:**
    *   **The "Just Right" Dimple:** The screw head is snug against the metal panel. The rubber gasket is compressed just enough to spread to the outer edge of the metal washer above it. It should not be bulging out or "pancaked". A slight dimple in the metal around the screw head is normal.
    *   **Appearance:** The screw is perfectly perpendicular to the roof surface.
*   **Incorrectly Driven Screw (Mistakes):**
    *   **Over-tightened:** The rubber gasket is squashed and visibly bulging out from under the metal washer. This can break the washer's seal. The dimple in the metal is deep and distorted. There might be a sound of the screw threads stripping in the wood.
    *   **Under-tightened:** There is a visible gap between the gasket and the metal panel. The character should be able to spin the washer with their fingers, which indicates a loose screw and a poor seal.
    *   **Angled Screw:** The screw head is tilted to one side. The gasket is compressed on one side and gapped on the other, creating an obvious entry point for water.

### 3.3 Sensory Details and Cues:
*   **Sound:**
    *   **Drilling:** A short, high-pitched *zzzziiippp* as the self-drilling tip cuts through the thin sheet metal.
    *   **Seating:** The sound deepens as the threads bite into the wood rafter. A correctly seated screw will have a distinct stop as the clutch on the screw gun disengages.
    *   **Stripping (Mistake):** A continuous, high-pitched spinning sound with no change in tone, indicating the screw is no longer gripping the wood.
*   **Feel:**
    *   **Correct:** The user feels the initial resistance of the metal, followed by the screw pulling itself into the wood. There is a clear tactile "stop" when the screw is fully seated and the clutch disengages.
    *   **Incorrect (Over-tightening):** The user feels the screw suddenly "give way" as the threads strip the wood, or a hard, jarring stop if the screw head bottoms out too aggressively.

### 3.4 Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):
*   **Using an Impact Driver:** While common in other construction, the *rat-a-tat-tat* sound and hammering motion of an impact driver is incorrect for this application and will likely damage the gaskets. Use the smooth whirring sound of a screw gun.
*   **Screws on the Ribs:** For most common salvaged panels (like corrugated or "5V" crimp), placing screws on the high ribs is an old-timer method that is now considered incorrect by most manufacturers for ensuring a tight seal. Screws go in the flat part where there is solid wood directly underneath.
*   **Perfectly Straight, Effortless Screws:** Show the character taking a moment to align the screw properly. Not every screw will go in perfectly on the first try. Occasionally show a screw being backed out and re-driven to correct an angle.
*   **Ignoring Swarf:** The small metal shavings created by drilling are called "swarf." A realistic portrayal would show the character brushing this swarf off the panel after driving a line of screws, as these shavings will rust and stain the roof if left on the surface.
