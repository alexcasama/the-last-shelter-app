## Technical Guide: Securing a Mortise and Tenon Joint with a Hand-Carved Oak Peg

This guide provides a detailed, step-by-step technical breakdown for generating a realistic video sequence of a craftsman securing a mortise and tenon joint by driving a hand-carved oak peg with a sledgehammer. The focus is on mechanical accuracy, physical realism, and the visual details of the process.

### **1. Preparation of the Oak Peg**

The process begins with the creation of the peg itself. Authenticity is key; a commercially produced dowel will look fake.

**Tools Required:**
*   **Froe and Mallet:** For splitting a straight-grained green oak block into blanks.
*   **Shaving Horse and Drawknife:** For shaping the peg.

**Physical Movements:**
*   **Splitting the Blank:** The craftsman places a froe on the end grain of a green oak block and strikes it firmly with a large wooden mallet. The body movement is confident and controlled, using the weight of the mallet to do the work. The split should follow the grain of the wood, resulting in a rough, rectangular blank.
*   **Shaping on the Shaving Horse:** The craftsman sits on the shaving horse, using their feet to clamp the oak blank. With a drawknife held in both hands, they pull the blade towards their body, peeling off shavings. The movements are rhythmic and efficient. The craftsman rotates the blank to first create a square, tapered peg, and then to chamfer the corners, resulting in an octagonal shape. The taper is crucial, with one end slightly wider than the other.

**Visual Cues:**
*   **The Oak Blank:** Initially, the blank is rough-sawn or split, with a visible grain. It should be "green" oak, meaning it's freshly cut and has a higher moisture content.
*   **Shaping Process:** Long, thin shavings of oak peel away with each pass of the drawknife. The surface of the peg transitions from rough to a tooled, faceted appearance. The final peg should be octagonal and tapered, not perfectly round.
*   **The Finished Peg:** The peg is approximately 250mm long and 19mm in diameter, with a distinct taper. One end is pointed or chamfered to aid insertion. The surface shows the subtle facets from the drawknife.

**Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):**
*   A perfectly cylindrical, smooth dowel. Real hand-carved pegs have slight irregularities.
*   Using dry, pale wood for the peg. Green oak has a richer, more vibrant color.
*   Short, jerky movements with the drawknife. The motion should be fluid and controlled.

### **2. The Drawboring Technique: Offsetting the Holes**

To create a powerful mechanical lock, the hole in the tenon is intentionally offset from the hole in the mortise. This is known as drawboring.

**Tools Required:**
*   **Brace and Auger Bit or a Drill Press with a Forstner Bit:** For boring the holes.
*   **Marking Gauge or Pencil:** To lay out the hole locations.

**Physical Movements:**
*   **Drilling the Mortise:** The two pieces of the joint are assembled. A hole is drilled through both sides of the mortise, passing through the tenon. The craftsman ensures the drill is perpendicular to the surface.
*   **Marking the Tenon:** The joint is then disassembled. The location of the hole on the tenon is marked.
*   **Offsetting the Hole:** A new mark is made on the tenon, slightly closer to the shoulder of the joint (typically by about 1/16th of an inch).
*   **Drilling the Tenon:** The hole is then drilled through the tenon at this new, offset mark.

**Visual Cues:**
*   **Assembled Joint:** The mortise and tenon should fit snugly together before any drilling.
*   **Offset Holes:** When the joint is reassembled, the hole through the mortise and the hole through the tenon will not perfectly align. Looking through the hole in the mortise, you will see that the hole in the tenon is partially obscured. This visual confirmation is a key element of realism.

**Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):**
*   The holes in the mortise and tenon aligning perfectly. This would just be a pinned joint, not a drawbored one, and would lack the cinching action.
*   The offset being too large, which could cause the peg or the tenon to break under pressure.

### **3. Driving the Peg with a Sledgehammer**

This is the climactic step where the joint is secured. The choice of a sledgehammer indicates a large-scale timber framing project.

**Tools Required:**
*   **Sledgehammer or a Large Wooden Mallet (also called a "Beetle"):** A heavy hammer is needed to drive the peg through the offset holes. For timber framing, a wooden sledgehammer is often used to avoid damaging the wood.

**Physical Movements:**
*   **Stance and Grip:** The craftsman takes a solid, balanced stance with feet shoulder-width apart. They grip the sledgehammer with both hands, one near the base of the handle and the other further up for control.
*   **The Swing:** The swing is powerful but controlled. It originates from the core and shoulders, not just the arms. The craftsman's eyes are focused on the head of the peg.
*   **The Impact:** The initial taps are lighter, to set the peg in the hole. As the peg begins to bite into the offset tenon hole, the swings become more forceful. There should be a clear sense of resistance as the peg aligns the holes and pulls the joint tight. The craftsman continues driving the peg until it is fully seated.

**Visual Cues:**
*   **Initial Entry:** The tapered end of the peg enters the hole in the mortise.
*   **Bending and Tightening:** As the peg is driven further, you should see the shoulder of the tenon visibly pull tight against the mortise. The peg itself may flex slightly under the immense pressure of aligning the two offset holes.
*   **Seating the Peg:** The peg is driven until it protrudes from the other side. The craftsman may leave the ends of the peg protruding to be trimmed later.
*   **Wood Compression:** There might be slight compression of the wood fibers around the entry and exit holes, indicating a very tight fit.

**Mistakes to Avoid (What Looks Fake):**
*   The peg sliding in too easily. There must be visible and audible resistance.
*   The joint not visibly tightening. The "draw" effect is the entire point of this technique.
*   Using a small, standard hammer. The mass of a sledgehammer or large mallet is necessary for this task.
*   Wild, uncontrolled swings. An experienced craftsman is powerful yet precise.
*   The peg breaking. While possible in reality, for a demonstration of success, the peg should hold. A well-made, straight-grained oak peg is incredibly strong.
*   Missing the peg and denting the surrounding timber. While this can happen, a clean strike demonstrates skill. If a miss is shown, it should be followed by a realistic reaction from the craftsman.
